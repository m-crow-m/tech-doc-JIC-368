David Foster Wallace

David Foster Wallace emerged as a revolutionary American writer after studying English and philosophy at Amherst College. His senior thesis became his first novel, "The Broom of the System." After briefly pursuing philosophy at Harvard and battling depression, he switched to creative writing at the University of Arizona.

Wallace's 1996 masterpiece "Infinite Jest" transformed American fiction with its ambitious scope and profound exploration of addiction and human connection. His non-fiction, including essays in "Consider the Lobster" and his famous "This Is Water" commencement address, demonstrated his unique blend of philosophical inquiry and cultural criticism.

Wallace influenced countless writers with his maximalist style that balanced intellectual complexity with emotional depth. His work addressed modern challenges: information overload, consumerism, and the search for authentic connection. His suicide in 2008 cut short a brilliant career, but his legacy as a literary innovator who fundamentally altered American literature endures.

Continue reading for a look into the literary works of Foster Wallace and the impact they have had on culture and writing.

Infinite Jest

(1996)

A sprawling narrative set in a near-future North America, following three main threads:

/The Incandenza family and Enfield Tennis Academy
/Don Gately and the Ennet House Drug and Alcohol Recovery House
/Québécois separatists seeking a lethally entertaining film

Cultural Impact

  • Revolutionized postmodern literature with its complex narrative structure
  • Predicted aspects of modern digital entertainment addiction
  • Influenced contemporary discussions about attention spans and entertainment
  • Created new standards for experimental fiction and footnote usage
  • Sparked ongoing debates about information overload
  • Referenced extensively in modern media and literature
  • Influenced treatment of mental health and addiction in fiction
  • Changed how contemporary authors approach novel length and complexity

The truest measure of David's genius wasn't his vocabulary or his footnotes or his syntactical gymnastics. It was his radical insistence that literature should help us become better, more compassionate human beings. It was his belief that fiction's highest calling is to remind us that everyone else is just as complex,

conflicted &

confused as we are.
